## 7 Common URL Slug Mistakes That Hurt Your SEO

Your URL slug might seem like a minor detail, but it plays a surprisingly important role in how search engines understand, rank, and display your pages. A poorly constructed slug can confuse crawlers, dilute your keyword relevance, and create a frustrating experience for users who actually read URLs before clicking.

The good news is that most slug mistakes are easy to avoid once you know what to look for. Here are the seven most common URL slug errors that could be silently hurting your SEO, along with practical fixes for each one.

### 1. Using Auto-Generated Slugs Without Editing Them

Most content management systems will automatically generate a slug from your page title or headline. While convenient, these auto-generated slugs are almost always too long, too messy, or both. They often include filler words, numeric IDs, or entire sentences crammed into the URL.

Search engines prefer concise, descriptive slugs. An overly long slug dilutes the weight of your target keywords and looks untrustworthy to users scanning search results. Slugs containing database IDs are even worse because they provide zero semantic value to either humans or crawlers.

**Bad:** `/blog/12845-here-is-why-you-should-always-take-the-time-to-optimize-your-url-slugs-for-seo`

**Good:** `/blog/optimize-url-slugs-seo`

**The fix:** Always manually review and edit your slug before publishing. Strip out filler words like "the," "is," "and," "here," and "why." Remove any auto-appended IDs. Aim for 3 to 5 meaningful words that capture the core topic of the page.

### 2. Including Dates in Slugs

Adding dates to your URL slugs is a trap that many bloggers and news sites fall into. The logic seems sound at first: organize content chronologically. But the reality is that a date-stamped slug signals to both users and search engines that the content is tied to a specific moment in time, which makes it look stale faster.

If you update an article written in 2021 with fresh data, the slug still screams "old content." Users browsing search results may skip over it entirely, and search engines may deprioritize it in favor of seemingly newer pages.

**Bad:** `/blog/2021/03/15/best-seo-practices`

**Good:** `/blog/best-seo-practices`

**The fix:** Keep dates out of your slugs entirely unless the content is genuinely date-specific, like an event recap or an annual report. Use your CMS's metadata or structured data to communicate publication and update dates to search engines instead.

### 3. Using Underscores Instead of Hyphens

This is one of the oldest slug mistakes in the book, and it still persists. Google has explicitly stated that it treats hyphens as word separators but treats underscores as word joiners. That means `best_seo_tips` is interpreted as a single token, "bestseotips," rather than three separate words.

The practical impact is that your page becomes harder to match against relevant search queries. You lose the keyword segmentation that hyphens naturally provide.

**Bad:** `/blog/best_seo_tips_for_beginners`

**Good:** `/blog/best-seo-tips-for-beginners`

**The fix:** Always use hyphens to separate words in your slugs. If you are migrating an older site that used underscores, set up 301 redirects from the old underscore URLs to the new hyphenated versions.

### 4. Leaving Special Characters and Accented Characters

Special characters like `&`, `%`, `@`, `!`, and accented characters like `e`, `n`, or `u` can wreak havoc on your URLs. Browsers and servers often encode these characters into percent-encoded sequences, turning a readable slug into an ugly string of `%C3%A9` codes. Some systems may even break the URL entirely, resulting in 404 errors.

Beyond technical issues, these characters make URLs harder to share, copy, and remember. A URL that looks garbled in a messaging app or email is far less likely to be clicked.

**Bad:** `/blog/cafe-resume-tips-&-tricks-for-ninos`

**Good:** `/blog/cafe-resume-tips-and-tricks-for-ninos`

**The fix:** Transliterate accented characters to their ASCII equivalents (e to e, n to n, u to u). Replace symbols like `&` with words like "and." Strip out any remaining special characters. A good slug generator tool will handle this automatically.

### 5. Making Slugs Too Long or Too Short

There is a sweet spot for slug length, and missing it in either direction hurts. Slugs that are too long get truncated in search results, look spammy, and dilute keyword relevance. Slugs that are too short, on the other hand, are often too vague to provide any meaningful context about the page's content.

A single-word slug like `/blog/seo` tells neither search engines nor users what specifically the page covers. A 15-word slug buries the important keywords in a sea of noise.

**Bad (too long):** `/blog/the-complete-and-ultimate-guide-to-every-single-seo-strategy-you-will-ever-need-in-your-life`

**Bad (too short):** `/blog/seo`

**Good:** `/blog/complete-seo-strategy-guide`

**The fix:** Aim for 3 to 5 words in most slugs. Each word should earn its place by adding meaningful context or a target keyword. Read the slug out loud. If it sounds like a sentence, it is too long. If it sounds like a label with no context, it is too short.

### 6. Changing Slugs After Publishing Without Redirects

Updating a slug after a page has been live is one of the most damaging mistakes you can make if you do not handle it correctly. The original URL may already have backlinks pointing to it, social shares referencing it, and search engine rankings built around it. Changing the slug without a redirect means all of that accumulated value vanishes overnight, replaced by 404 errors.

Every broken link is a missed opportunity for traffic and a negative signal to search engines about your site's reliability.

**Bad:** Changing `/blog/seo-audit-checklist` to `/blog/ultimate-seo-audit-guide` with no redirect.

**Good:** Changing the slug AND setting up a 301 redirect from `/blog/seo-audit-checklist` to `/blog/ultimate-seo-audit-guide`.

**The fix:** If you must change a slug, always set up a permanent 301 redirect from the old URL to the new one. Test the redirect to make sure it works. Keep a log of all slug changes so you can troubleshoot any traffic drops. Better yet, get the slug right the first time and avoid changing it altogether.

### 7. Keyword Stuffing in Slugs

It can be tempting to pack every relevant keyword variation into your slug to maximize search visibility. But just like keyword stuffing in body content, cramming keywords into your slug is a tactic that backfires. Search engines recognize this pattern as manipulative, and it can trigger spam filters or ranking penalties.

Beyond algorithmic risks, a stuffed slug looks untrustworthy to users. A URL that reads like a list of search terms rather than a logical page address signals low-quality content.

**Bad:** `/blog/seo-tips-seo-tricks-seo-hacks-best-seo-strategies-seo`

**Good:** `/blog/actionable-seo-tips`

**The fix:** Include your primary keyword once in the slug. That is usually enough. If you can naturally include a secondary keyword without making the slug awkward, fine, but never force it. The slug should read like a concise, human-friendly description of the page, not a keyword dump.

## The Bottom Line

URL slugs are a small but consequential part of your SEO strategy. Each mistake on this list is avoidable with a few seconds of thought before you hit publish. Take the time to craft clean, concise, hyphenated slugs with one clear keyword and no unnecessary clutter. Your search rankings, your users, and your future self will thank you.
