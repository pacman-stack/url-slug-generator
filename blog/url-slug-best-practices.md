## 10 URL Slug Best Practices for Better Rankings

URL slugs are one of the most overlooked elements in search engine optimization. That short string of text at the end of your URL might seem insignificant, but it plays a meaningful role in how search engines understand your content and how users decide whether to click on your link. A well-crafted slug improves crawlability, reinforces topical relevance, and gives searchers a clear preview of what they will find on the page.

Whether you are launching a new site or cleaning up an existing one, these 10 best practices will help you write slugs that support better rankings and higher click-through rates.

### 1. Keep Slugs Short (3-5 Words)

Shorter slugs are easier for search engines to parse and for users to read and remember. Research consistently shows that shorter URLs tend to correlate with higher rankings. Aim for three to five meaningful words that capture the core topic of the page. Anything longer starts to dilute keyword relevance and makes the URL harder to share.

A concise slug also displays better in search engine results pages. Long URLs get truncated, which can reduce trust and click-through rates.

- **Good:** `example.com/url-slug-best-practices`
- **Bad:** `example.com/the-top-10-best-practices-for-writing-url-slugs-in-2024-for-seo`

### 2. Use Hyphens as Separators

Hyphens are the universally accepted word separator in URLs. Google's own documentation explicitly recommends hyphens over underscores, spaces (which become `%20`), or any other character. Search engines treat hyphens as spaces between words, which means `url-slug-tips` is read as three distinct words. Underscores, on the other hand, join words together, so `url_slug_tips` may be interpreted as a single token.

Stick with hyphens every time, no exceptions.

- **Good:** `example.com/seo-friendly-urls`
- **Bad:** `example.com/seo_friendly_urls`
- **Bad:** `example.com/seo%20friendly%20urls`

### 3. Include Target Keywords

Your URL slug is a ranking signal. Including your primary target keyword in the slug tells both search engines and users exactly what the page is about. Place the most important keyword as close to the beginning of the slug as possible for maximum impact.

That said, do not stuff multiple keywords into a single slug. One primary keyword phrase is sufficient. Keyword stuffing in URLs can look spammy and may trigger search engine penalties.

- **Good:** `example.com/email-marketing-guide`
- **Bad:** `example.com/page-12847`
- **Bad:** `example.com/email-marketing-tips-email-strategy-email-guide`

### 4. Use Lowercase Only

URLs are case-sensitive on most web servers. That means `example.com/SEO-Tips` and `example.com/seo-tips` can be treated as two entirely different pages. This creates duplicate content issues that confuse search engines and split your link equity between two URLs.

Always use lowercase letters in your slugs to avoid this problem entirely. Most content management systems handle this automatically, but it is worth double-checking, especially if you create URLs manually.

- **Good:** `example.com/content-marketing-tips`
- **Bad:** `example.com/Content-Marketing-Tips`

### 5. Remove Stop Words

Stop words are common words like "a," "the," "is," "and," "of," "for," and "in." These words add length to your slug without adding any SEO value. Search engines largely ignore them when analyzing URLs, so removing them keeps your slug lean and focused on the words that matter.

Use your judgment here. If removing a stop word makes the slug unreadable or changes its meaning, keep it. The goal is clarity, not blind removal.

- **Good:** `example.com/increase-website-traffic`
- **Bad:** `example.com/how-to-increase-the-traffic-on-your-website`

### 6. Avoid Special Characters

Special characters such as `&`, `%`, `@`, `!`, `=`, `+`, and brackets have no place in URL slugs. Many of these characters carry special meaning in URLs (query parameters, encoding, fragment identifiers) and can cause parsing errors, broken links, or unexpected behavior. They also make URLs look unprofessional and harder to share.

Stick to lowercase letters, numbers, and hyphens. That is all you need for a clean, functional slug.

- **Good:** `example.com/top-10-seo-tools`
- **Bad:** `example.com/top-10-seo-tools!-(2024-edition)&updated`

### 7. Make Slugs Descriptive

A good slug gives the reader a clear idea of the page content before they even click. Think of it as a short summary or label. Someone should be able to look at the URL in a search result or shared link and know exactly what to expect.

Avoid generic or auto-generated slugs that use IDs, dates, or random strings. These tell neither the user nor the search engine anything useful about the page.

- **Good:** `example.com/beginner-yoga-poses`
- **Bad:** `example.com/post-384729`
- **Bad:** `example.com/2024/11/10/untitled`

### 8. Use Canonical URLs for Duplicates

Sometimes the same content is accessible through multiple URLs. This can happen with tracking parameters, session IDs, print-friendly versions, or pagination. When search engines find duplicate content across different URLs, they are forced to guess which one is the "real" page, and they do not always guess correctly.

Set a canonical tag (`rel="canonical"`) on every page that points to the preferred URL. This consolidates ranking signals onto a single slug and prevents dilution. If you use a URL slug generator or CMS, check that canonical tags are being set automatically.

- **Good:** `<link rel="canonical" href="https://example.com/running-shoes-guide" />`
- **Bad:** Letting `example.com/running-shoes-guide`, `example.com/running-shoes-guide?ref=twitter`, and `example.com/running-shoes-guide?utm_source=newsletter` all compete with each other.

### 9. Don't Change Slugs After Publishing (or Use Redirects)

Once a page is live and indexed, its URL starts accumulating backlinks, social shares, and search engine trust. Changing the slug breaks all of those existing links and resets much of the authority you have built. Visitors who follow old links will hit a 404 error, which damages both user experience and SEO.

If you absolutely must change a slug, always set up a 301 redirect from the old URL to the new one. A 301 redirect tells search engines that the page has permanently moved and passes the majority of link equity to the new address. Never change a slug without implementing this redirect.

- **Good:** Old slug `example.com/seo-basics` 301 redirects to `example.com/seo-fundamentals`
- **Bad:** Changing `example.com/seo-basics` to `example.com/seo-fundamentals` with no redirect, resulting in a 404 error.

### 10. Match Slug to Page Content

Your slug should accurately reflect what the page delivers. If your slug says `email-marketing-guide` but the page is actually a product landing page for email software, users will feel misled and search engines will notice the mismatch. This disconnect hurts your bounce rate, dwell time, and ultimately your rankings.

Write your slug after you finalize the page content. Read through the finished piece, identify its core topic in three to five words, and use that as your slug. This ensures alignment between the URL, the title tag, the headings, and the body content, which sends strong, consistent relevance signals to search engines.

- **Good:** `example.com/how-to-brew-cold-coffee` for a tutorial on brewing cold coffee
- **Bad:** `example.com/best-coffee` for a page that is actually a review of coffee machines

## Putting It All Together

Crafting the perfect URL slug is not complicated, but it does require intentional effort. Before you publish any page, run through this quick checklist:

1. Is the slug three to five words long?
2. Does it use hyphens between words?
3. Does it include the target keyword?
4. Is everything lowercase?
5. Have stop words been removed where appropriate?
6. Are there no special characters?
7. Does the slug clearly describe the content?
8. Is a canonical tag in place?
9. If this replaces an old URL, is a 301 redirect configured?
10. Does the slug match what the page actually delivers?

If you can answer yes to all ten questions, you have a slug that is optimized for both search engines and human readers. Small details like these compound over time, and clean, well-structured URLs are one of the easiest wins in SEO.

Start applying these practices today, and you will see the difference in how your pages perform in search results.
